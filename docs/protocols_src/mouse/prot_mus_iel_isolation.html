Mouse Small Intestinal Intraepithelial Lymphocyte (IEL) Isolation

Abdullah Lab, IMMEI, University Hospital Bonn

Isolation of intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs) from the mouse small intestine for flow cytometry and immunophenotyping, using DTT/EDTA epithelial stripping, mechanical agitation, and Percoll density centrifugation.
Published

April 8, 2026

Protocol ID: MUS-IEL-ISO
Version: v1.2
Author: Dillon Corvino

Purpose

This protocol describes the isolation of intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs) from the mouse small intestine for downstream flow cytometry. IELs reside within the intestinal epithelial layer, intercalated between epithelial cells, and are physically distinct from lamina propria lymphocytes (LPLs).

The protocol uses DTT/EDTA-based epithelial stripping combined with mechanical agitation to release the epithelial fraction, followed by Percoll density centrifugation to enrich lymphocytes. An optional ACK RBC lysis step is included.

Background: gut anatomy, immune compartments, and isolation logic

This section provides an anatomical and immunological reference for the intestinal tract relevant to IEL isolation. It is intended as orientation for researchers new to gut immunology and as a practical guide for experimental design decisions (segment choice, compartment targeting, downstream assay selection).

Overview of the intestinal tract

The mouse intestinal tract runs approximately 30 cm in total and is divided into the small intestine and large intestine, each with distinct anatomy, function, and immune composition.

Small intestine

The small intestine comprises three contiguous segments, running proximal to distal:

Segment Approximate proportion Key anatomical features Primary function
Duodenum ~5% of SI length Short; receives bile and pancreatic secretions; long villi (villus:crypt ~3–5:1) Digestion of fats, carbohydrates, protein; iron absorption
Jejunum ~40% of SI length Longest segment; most active absorption; longest and densest villi Primary nutrient absorption
Ileum ~55% of SI length Shorter villi; highest density of Peyer’s patches; terminal segment before cecum Vitamin B12 and bile acid reabsorption; immune sampling

The small intestinal epithelium is organised into finger-like villi projecting into the lumen (absent in the large intestine) and crypts of Lieberkühn at the base of the villi. This villus–crypt architecture dramatically increases luminal surface area and is the structural context within which IELs reside.

Large intestine

The large intestine follows the small intestine and comprises:

Segment Notes
Cecum Blind-ended pouch at the SI–LI junction; largest microbial reservoir; enlarged in germ-free mice
Colon (proximal → distal) Lacks villi; has crypts only; much thicker mucus layer than SI; primary site of water reabsorption
Rectum / anus Terminal segment

The large intestine has a far higher microbial load than the small intestine (bacterial density increases ~1,000-fold from duodenum to colon) and a correspondingly distinct immune environment dominated by regulatory and microbiota-responsive cells.

Note

IEL density is substantially higher in the small intestine than the large intestine. The large intestine has approximately 5–10× fewer IELs per epithelial cell than the small intestine under steady-state conditions. This protocol targets small intestinal IELs only.

Wall architecture and immune compartments

The intestinal wall is organised into concentric layers. Moving from the lumen outward:

Lumen
  │
  ▼
Epithelium          ← IELs live here (within the epithelial layer)
  │
Basement membrane
  │
Lamina propria      ← LPLs, DCs, macrophages, mast cells, ILCs
  │
Muscularis mucosae
  │
Submucosa           ← Nerve plexus, blood and lymphatic vessels
  │
Muscularis externa  ← Smooth muscle (inner circular + outer longitudinal)
  │
Serosa / mesentery

For immunological purposes, the two most important layers are the epithelium and the lamina propria.

Epithelial layer and the IEL compartment

The intestinal epithelium is a single-cell-thick monolayer of epithelial cells (primarily enterocytes) that lines the entire luminal surface. IELs are embedded within this monolayer, sitting between neighbouring epithelial cells with their cell bodies typically below the tight junctions but above the basement membrane. They are in direct contact with the epithelium and sample the luminal environment.

In the small intestine, there is approximately 1 IEL per 5–10 epithelial cells, making the gut epithelium one of the largest T cell compartments in the body by cell number. IELs are long-lived, largely non-recirculating tissue-resident cells.

How to access IELs: Because IELs are mechanically held within the epithelial layer by integrin interactions (primarily αEβ7/CD103), they are released by disrupting epithelial junctions with EDTA (chelates calcium, destabilising E-cadherin-based junctions) and DTT (reduces disulfide bonds in mucus), combined with mechanical agitation. This is the stripping step in this protocol.

Lamina propria and the LPL compartment

The lamina propria is the loose connective tissue layer immediately beneath the epithelium, separated from it by the basement membrane. It contains:

  • T cells — predominantly CD4+ T helper cells; also CD8+ effector T cells; Tregs
  • B cells and plasma cells — IgA-secreting plasma cells are abundant; responsible for secretory IgA production
  • Dendritic cells — CD11b+CD8α− DCs predominate in the small intestinal LP; important for antigen sampling and T cell priming
  • Macrophages — major phagocytic and tolerogenic population; form close contacts with the epithelium
  • Mast cells — involved in helminth responses and barrier defence
  • Innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) — ILC1, ILC2, ILC3 populations present; ILC3s produce IL-22 for barrier maintenance

How to access LPLs: The basement membrane must be disrupted after removing the epithelial layer. This requires enzymatic digestion of the remaining tissue with collagenase (± DNase), typically at 37 °C with agitation. LPL isolation is a separate workflow from IEL isolation and is not covered in this protocol. The intestinal pieces remaining after the stripping and shake steps in this protocol can be used directly as input for LP digestion.

Peyer’s patches

Peyer’s patches are organised lymphoid follicles embedded in the intestinal wall, predominantly in the ileum. They are visible as pale, dome-shaped nodules on the anti-mesenteric surface. They contain:

  • Dense B cell follicles (B220+ B cells)
  • Germinal centre reactions for IgA class switching
  • T cell zones with CD4+ T cells
  • Specialised M cells (microfold cells) in the overlying follicle-associated epithelium for antigen transcytosis

Peyer’s patches are not part of the IEL or LP compartments and must be removed before processing. If retained, the B cell-rich follicular content heavily contaminates IEL preparations and distorts flow cytometry data.

Segment-specific immune cell composition

Small intestinal IEL subsets by segment

IEL subset distribution is not uniform along the small intestine. The two major functional categories of IELs are:

  • Natural IELs (nIELs) — arise from thymic precursors; present in germ-free mice; include TCRγδ+ IELs and TCRαβ+ CD8αα+ IELs. More abundant in the proximal SI (duodenum/jejunum).
  • Peripheral/induced IELs (pIELs) — arise from conventional peripheral T cells that downregulate CD8αβ and upregulate CD8αα (or become CD4+CD8αα+) upon entry into the epithelium. Enriched in the ileum, driven by microbiota-dependent signals including high MHC-II and PD-L1 expression in that region.
IEL subset TCR CD8 form Segment enriched Microbiota-dependent?
nIEL type a TCRγδ CD8αα or DN Proximal SI No (present in GF)
nIEL type b TCRαβ CD8αα Proximal SI No (present in GF)
pIEL (induced) TCRαβ CD4+CD8αα+ Ileum Yes (absent/low in GF)
pIEL (induced) TCRαβ CD8αβ → CD8αα Ileum Yes

For most standard CD8αα IEL analyses, the whole small intestine is used to maximise yield. Segment-specific harvest is appropriate only when subset enrichment is the experimental goal.

Large intestinal immune environment

The colon has a distinct immune composition compared with the small intestine:

  • Thicker, stratified mucus layer — creates a physical exclusion zone separating bacteria from the epithelium; much more prominent than in SI.
  • Higher regulatory T cell frequency — the colon is more tolerogenic overall.
  • Microbiota-dependent immune cell shaping — colonic immune cell composition is more severely disrupted in germ-free conditions than SI.
  • Fewer IELs and DCs — under homeostatic conditions; both are recruited upon inflammation.
  • No villi — crypts only; different epithelial cell organisation affects IEL numbers and accessibility.

Note

This protocol uses the whole small intestine as the default. If segment-specific enrichment is required (e.g. ileum only for pIEL studies, or proximal SI for nIEL studies), dissect and process each segment separately. Label clearly: duodenum begins immediately distal to the pyloric sphincter; the ileum ends at the ileocecal junction; the jejunum is the long intervening segment.

Critical notes (read before starting)

  • Remove all Peyer’s patches before processing — they are B-cell-rich and will contaminate the IEL fraction if retained.
  • Open the intestine longitudinally and wash thoroughly before stripping — residual luminal contents and mucus inhibit stripping efficiency and reduce recovery.
  • DTT reduces disulfide bonds in mucus and epithelial junctions, improving IEL release; do not exceed recommended concentration or incubation time as overdose can reduce viability.
  • The stripping step uses a magnetic stirrer at 37 °C — this is a key feature of this protocol and improves IEL yield compared with passive incubation.
  • The manual shake step after stripping further mechanically dislodges IELs; perform it vigorously.
  • Percoll enrichment is essential — without it, the preparation will be heavily contaminated with epithelial cells.
  • Process samples promptly; keep samples on ice between active steps.

Approximate timing

  • Intestine harvest, opening, and washing: 15–20 min per mouse
  • DTT/EDTA stripping (37 °C stir): 20 min
  • Manual shake steps (×2): 5 min
  • Centrifugation and Percoll enrichment: 40–50 min
  • ACK lysis, wash, and final resuspension: 15 min

Total approximate time: ~1.5–2 hours per mouse (batching 2–4 mice in parallel is feasible with practice)

Procedure

STEP 1 – Preparation and setup

  1. Prepare all buffers fresh (see Buffers section). Pre-warm Strip Medium to 37 °C in a water bath. Keep all other buffers on ice.
  2. Set up a magnetic stir plate at 37 °C with a stir bar and a beaker — this is used for the stripping step.
  3. Prepare a metal strainer over a large collection beaker for filtering after stripping.
  4. Label 50 mL tubes for each mouse: one for stripping collection, one for downstream processing.
  5. Pre-cool the centrifuge to 4 °C.
  6. Prepare 35% Percoll working solution (see Buffers section) at room temperature.

STEP 2 – Intestine harvest and preparation

  1. Euthanise the mouse by an approved method.
  2. Open the abdominal cavity and locate the small intestine.
  3. Excise the entire small intestine, cutting just distal to the pyloric sphincter (stomach) and at the ileocecal junction.
  4. Remove all attached mesenteric fat and connective tissue along the intestinal length.
  5. Identify and excise all Peyer’s patches (pale, dome-shaped lymphoid nodules on the anti-mesenteric border). Cut them away and discard.
Warning

Peyer’s patches must be removed completely. They contain a distinct lymphoid population that will contaminate the IEL fraction.

  1. Cut the intestine open longitudinally along its entire length using fine scissors.
  2. Wash the opened intestine once with 3% Medium and then once with PBS to remove luminal contents and mucus.
  3. After washing, transfer the intestinal tissue to a 50 mL Falcon tube with a small volume of 3% Medium to keep it moist until the next step.
Note

Thorough washing after opening is critical. Residual mucus reduces stripping efficiency.

STEP 3 – DTT/EDTA stripping (37 °C magnetic stir)

  1. Cut the washed intestinal tissue into approximately 5 pieces of roughly equal length.
  2. Place the pieces into the pre-warmed beaker (on the magnetic stir plate at 37 °C) containing 20 mL Strip Medium (RPMI/3% FCS + 1 mM DTT + 5 mM EDTA, pre-warmed to 37 °C).
  3. Add a magnetic stir bar to the beaker. Cover with aluminium foil.
  4. Incubate at 37 °C for 20 minutes with continuous stirring at 250 rpm.
Warning

Do not exceed the 20-minute incubation or increase DTT concentration above 1 mM. Over-exposure to DTT reduces leukocyte viability.

  1. After incubation, strain the contents through the metal strainer into the collection beaker to collect the stripped epithelial fraction.
  2. Keep the intestinal pieces on the strainer for the manual shake step (STEP 4).

STEP 4 – Manual shake washes

The mechanical shake step dislodges residual IELs from the tissue and maximises recovery.

Shake round 1

  1. Transfer the intestinal pieces from the metal strainer into a 50 mL Falcon tube containing 10 mL Shake Medium (RPMI + 1% glutamine + 1% Pen/Strep + 2% HEPES + 2 mM EDTA).
  2. Cap the tube and shake vigorously by hand for 30 seconds.
  3. Pour the tube contents back through the metal strainer into the collection beaker.

Shake round 2

  1. Return the intestinal pieces to the Falcon tube with a fresh 10 mL Shake Medium.
  2. Shake vigorously for 30 seconds as before.
  3. Pour back through the metal strainer into the collection beaker.

The collection beaker now contains the pooled stripping and shake fractions. The intestinal tissue pieces can be discarded or retained for LP isolation.

STEP 5 – Transfer and centrifugation

  1. Transfer the total cell suspension from the collection beaker into a 50 mL Falcon tube.
  2. Centrifuge at 400 g, 4 °C, 10 minutes.
Note

The colleague protocol reports 550 g for this step. Standard published IEL protocols use 400 × g for this wash spin. Confirm with your local centrifuge rotor to determine the exact rpm corresponding to your instrument settings.

  1. Discard the supernatant.

STEP 6 – 35% Percoll enrichment

  1. Resuspend the pellet in 10 mL of 35% Percoll.
Note

On Percoll percentage: This protocol uses 35% Percoll as per the collaborator protocol. Literature precedent for mouse IEL isolation varies: single-density pellet-through protocols typically use 35–44%, while discontinuous gradient protocols use 44%/67% (interface collection) for higher purity. The 44/67% discontinuous gradient consistently yields 30–80% CD45+ lymphocytes post-separation and is used in the most widely cited murine IEL protocols (Lefrancois & Lycke 2001; Bhatt & Bhatt 2022 PMC8128104). A 37% single-density pellet-through (the concentration used in the lab for liver) should also work for IEL and is compatible with this workflow; lymphocytes pellet while many epithelial cells and debris remain in the supernatant. If purity is insufficient with 35–37%, consider switching to the 44/67% discontinuous gradient approach.

  1. Centrifuge at 800 g, 25 minutes, room temperature (20–22 °C):
    • Acceleration: moderate (acc 7)
    • Brake: minimal (br 1)
Warning

Do not use high brake — abrupt deceleration disrupts the interface and reduces enrichment.

  1. After centrifugation:
    • Lymphocytes pellet at the bottom.
    • Epithelial cells, debris, and dead cells remain in the supernatant.
  2. Carefully aspirate and discard the supernatant in one smooth motion.
  3. Wash the pellet: resuspend in PBS, centrifuge at 400 g, 4 °C, 10 minutes. Discard supernatant.

STEP 7 – ACK RBC lysis (optional)

Note

RBC contamination in IEL preparations is generally low because the intestinal epithelial compartment contains few red blood cells. This step can often be omitted, particularly if a Percoll gradient has been performed. Include if the pellet appears visibly red.

  1. Resuspend the cell pellet in 7 mL ACK lysis buffer.
  2. Incubate for approximately 10 minutes at room temperature.
  3. Pass the suspension through a 70 µm cell strainer into a fresh 50 mL tube.
  4. Top up to 30 mL with PBS.
  5. Centrifuge at 400 g, 4 °C, 10 minutes.
  6. Discard the supernatant.

STEP 8 – Final resuspension and staining

  1. Resuspend the cell pellet in 300 µL PBS.
  2. Count cells and assess viability as required.
  3. Use 100 µL of suspension per flow cytometry panel.
  4. Add 100 µL antibody master mix and stain for 20 minutes (on ice, protected from light, as per staining SOP).
  5. After staining, resuspend in 200 µL PBS for acquisition.
Tip

Expected yield from one small intestine: approximately 3–6 × 10^6 total IEL from an 8–12-week-old naïve C57BL/6 mouse after Percoll enrichment (literature estimate; actual yield depends on mouse age, strain, and protocol execution). Contaminating epithelial cells can be excluded in flow cytometry using EpCAM (CD326) in the dump channel.

Notes on Percoll percentage and protocol variants

35% vs 37% vs 40–44% for IEL isolation

The Percoll concentration used for IEL enrichment varies across published protocols:

  • 35% — used by some groups (including this collaborator protocol) as a single-density pellet-through. Lymphocytes pellet; many epithelial cells remain in the supernatant. Simpler but lower purity.
  • 37% — the lab standard for liver leukocyte enrichment (BUF-PERC-37-001). Compatible with IEL isolation and can be used as a practical alternative to 35% without changing the isotonic Percoll stock preparation.
  • 40–44% — more widely used in published murine IEL protocols. Higher Percoll density improves separation of lymphocytes from epithelial cells and increases preparation purity.
  • 44%/67% discontinuous gradient — the gold standard in the most widely cited protocols (Lefrancois & Lycke 2001; Bhatt & Bhatt 2022). IELs are collected from the 44/67% interface rather than as a pellet, yielding 30–80% CD45+ purity. Requires underlaying 67% Percoll and more careful technique but gives the cleanest preparations.

Practical recommendation: Start with 35% (as in this protocol, matching the collaborator method). If purity is insufficient for your flow panel or gating (assess by EpCAM vs CD45 plot), escalate to a 44/67% discontinuous gradient.

Safety (brief)

  • All animal procedures must comply with approved animal licences and institutional regulations.
  • Wear appropriate PPE (lab coat, gloves, eye protection) when handling animals, tissues, and reagents.
  • DTT is a reducing agent with a strong odour; handle in a well-ventilated area and avoid inhalation.
  • Dispose of biological waste and sharps according to institutional biosafety guidelines.
